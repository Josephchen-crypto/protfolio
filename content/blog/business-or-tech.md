Business, or Stick With the Tech Path?

I've been thinking about this for the better part of a year. I don't have a clean answer yet. But I made a choice, so let me write it down before I forget again.

## Tried Everything, Ended Up Where I Started

Since 2025, I've been exploring the idea of becoming a "super individual." I tried a few different directions along the way. Most of them hit a wall pretty fast — too hard, too slow to make any money. I'd give up after about a month.

This year I finally circled back to the start. The fastest way to put cash in the bank is still the same as always: write apps.

But here's the thing. I'm completely bored of writing apps.

I've been doing this for ten years. The Chinese app market leaves me cold. Interfaces copy each other, features barely change. My old stack is muscle memory by now. Writing code feels like nothing.

That changed recently. I wanted to give blockchain development a shot.

## Blockchain Got Me Excited Again

There's something about blockchain I can't quite put into words. It feels mysterious. It feels cool.

The idea that anyone can take back control of their own money, that the global financial system could be more connected, more efficient, with way more use cases — that idea hooked me. I think this tech will spread the way smartphones did in 2011. It will change the world.

I love tech. I love digging into tech. I want to be part of the team that changes the world, even if my contribution is small.

So I planned to ease into blockchain through Android. Start with apps, build up from there.

My first instinct was to use my old XML view layer to spin up a demo fast. Then I caught myself — if I'm starting over anyway, why not use the best tools available?

I opened the Google developer site. I realized I hadn't opened it in almost five years. When I finally did, the Android I knew was unrecognizable.

The new stack made everything easier. Code got cleaner. Problems that used to be hard were suddenly trivial.

I love Compose. It feels a lot like Flutter — I've shipped apps in Flutter before — but I prefer Kotlin over Dart. Purely personal preference, no shade on Dart.

I built a few demos, then had AI gather the reference material. To keep AI from hallucinating wrong info, I set up a hard rule: every claim in my notes has to point to an official doc. That way I can read fast and absorb knowledge without second-guessing every line.

Honestly, most of my code gets written by AI now. If I don't use it, I can't keep up. The reason I still bother learning the stack myself is that you can't direct AI well if you don't understand the tech it's writing. You have to know enough to tell it which tool fits which job.

## Then Something Kind of Dramatic Happened

I was still planning to push into overseas app markets.

Some background first. My older sister and older brother both run businesses. They do textile fabric trading — connecting textile mills with clothing brands that need specific fabrics. My hometown is a national textile hub. Huge volumes of semi-finished fabric come out of there. We've got supply-chain advantages you could call global.

My brother got in early and made serious money. My older sister left the real estate industry after it cooled down — she'd been in it for ten years — and joined my brother in the textile trade.

Why does this matter?

Because the domestic mobile internet market, especially on the client side, has been squeezed hard by frontend. Native Android roles in China are scarce now. And the workplace is brutally competitive. My body couldn't take the pace anymore. That's why I left my last job to try other things. None of those worked out. I kept hitting the same wall: too hard, too slow.

I didn't start touching AI seriously until late 2025.

Even now, I haven't fully committed to a direction. But I can feel it — I've been knocking ideas off the list one by one. Getting closer to what I actually want.

Then my brother and sister reached out. They wanted me to join their business. 😂

## I Know I Can Say No

But they were worried about me. I'd been out of work for a full year, burning through savings. That doesn't last. They wanted to help me get back to earning.

The catch is the road is long. Early income would be low, the work is exhausting, and it means dealing with people. I really hate office politics — those schemes cooked up by schemers to torment everyone else. I'm a tech person at heart. I don't do well around people who play mind games.

But if I join the family business, I'd have to deal with factory folks and clients. They're sharp. They read people fast.

My sister thought about this too. That's why she told me to think it through before deciding.

## No Framework, Just Grit

Honestly, I'm foggy on which path to pick. I don't have a clean mental model to weigh the pros and cons. Too many variables. My head spins.

So I went online, looked for decision-making books, fed them to AI, extracted the methods, and had AI generate a skill to help me decide. The conclusion AI gave me was roughly what I already suspected.

The first question I kept coming back to was: how long will this last? If I commit, become an expert, will some tech shift or policy change wipe out everything I've built?

On that point, I lean toward the business. As long as civilization exists and the world doesn't end, people need clothes. My read is that the textile industry only iterates and improves — it never disappears.

Tech, on the other hand, I've felt it firsthand. Anything that doesn't tie to real-world productive assets gets obsolete fast. And the learning curve is brutal — you have to keep chasing the latest thing.

## I Accepted What AI Told Me

Looking at the real-world factors, maybe I should view what comes next from a different angle. Stepping out of your comfort zone is good for growth. The process will be painful. There will be problems.

But I'm not throwing out the original plan entirely. I still need a Plan-B. If the business doesn't work out — and it takes real patience and persistence in the early stages — I'll still carve out a smaller, focused block of time to keep going on my own path.

I probably won't go into blockchain development anymore, even though I'm fascinated by it. I might pivot toward the non-technical side of things. Overseas apps, for instance — keep exploring overseas app dev and operations with AI assistance.

## One More Thing That's Been on My Mind

Since graduation, there's been something I keep coming back to. I originally wanted to go deeper into 3D game modeling — but family circumstances got in the way. I've always dreamed of making a sci-fi short film. For over a decade, that unfinished dream has been poking at me.

That dream can wait a little longer. Maybe I'll only get to it between 45 and 55 — or maybe sooner. Hard to say.
